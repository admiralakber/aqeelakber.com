A solo bit on NEAT, POET, and MAP-Elites — evolutionary algorithms that grow neural networks and their wiring, not just weights. Aqeel walks through a weekend experiment where simple creatures evolve to recognise images they themselves produce, and how symmetry and low-energy constraints start to look uncannily like life.

Watch the full episode below — also on the podcast feed wherever you listen.

In this episode

  • A long-deferred weekend returns to NEAT—the evolutionary algorithm that grows a neural network's wiring, not merely its weights.
  • The fitness task is deliberately awkward: each creature sees an image it produced and must infer the DNA sequence that built both the picture and the brain.
  • Anyone with a browser can join a decentralised swarm over WebAssembly and PartyKit, evolving populations in parallel rather than on one machine.
  • POET and MAP-Elites linger in the background—algorithms built for shifting environments and robots that still walk when a leg disappears.
  • Attention, Hebbian learning, and neuromodulation help, yet one difficulty behaves like a halting problem: knowing how long your own genome is from a glimpse of yourself.
  • Symmetry and low-energy constraints enter as simple rules—and the search begins producing forms that feel less like diagrams and more like something else.

The conversation goes further than a list can — watch or listen above.
